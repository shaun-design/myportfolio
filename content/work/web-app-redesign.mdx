## Overview

A consumer productivity app had strong retention among power users but terrible activation for new ones. My task was to redesign the onboarding flow and core UI to improve first-week engagement without alienating the existing base.

## The Challenge

The app had been built incrementally over three years by engineers without a dedicated designer. The result was a UI that was powerful but difficult to learn — new users churned within days, before experiencing the features that made existing users loyal.

## Process

### Research

I conducted 12 user interviews split evenly between new signups (within the first 7 days) and long-term users (6+ months). The patterns were clear:

- New users didn't know where to start or what to do first
- The information hierarchy was inconsistent across the app
- Key actions were buried in nested menus that power users had memorized

### Design

I used the research to drive a focused redesign with three priorities:

1. **Simplified navigation** — collapsed secondary nav, surfaced the 4 most common actions at the top level
2. **Progressive disclosure** — hid advanced features behind progressive entry points rather than showing everything upfront
3. **Improved empty states** — replaced generic empty screens with contextual prompts that guided users toward their first meaningful action

I worked in Figma, iterating on high-fidelity prototypes before any code was written. We tested with 8 users across two rounds before locking the design.

### Implementation

I worked directly in the codebase using React and Tailwind, shipping the redesign over six weeks alongside a small engineering team. Having designers who can ship code dramatically shortened the feedback loop.

## Results

- 7-day activation rate improved by 34%
- Support tickets related to navigation dropped by 45%
- Average session length increased from 4.2 to 6.8 minutes

## Takeaways

The biggest win wasn't a specific design decision — it was tightening the loop between design and engineering. When the designer is also shipping pull requests, you stop playing telephone and start solving problems directly.
